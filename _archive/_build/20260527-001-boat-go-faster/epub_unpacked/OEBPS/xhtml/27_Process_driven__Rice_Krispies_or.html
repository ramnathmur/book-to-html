Process driven: Rice Krispies or Shreddies?

Ben: “If you want to win, you need to forget about winning.”

Chapter summary

Your best chance of getting great results is to stop focusing on results! Start focusing on the process that will get you great results and success will take care of itself.

How do you become process driven?

1.Get curious about the recipe

2.Focus your attention

3.Measure success

Do you feel under pressure to get results? Maybe you have work targets you must deliver or deadlines and budgets to meet? Maybe you’ve got a sporting personal best to achieve or maybe you are hunting for a new house or new job? Whatever the result you want, surely there are some secrets to steal from the British rowing 8+, because winning was their whole purpose?

Ben: “Don’t focus on the result; focus on the process that will get you the result.”

The crew’s coach, Martin, introduced this idea of being process-driven from his background in engineering. Martin reasoned that the crew were not going to win Gold by concentrating really hard on winning Gold. They would win it by concentrating really hard on using the best methods, by executing perfect strokes with a devastating rhythm and power, by developing a strong mindset and a strong team.

By following Martin’s logic we not only increase our chances of winning, we learn faster too. We might get lucky and win without concentrating on the process, but a successful outcome is just a fluke unless we know how we made it happen. When we know how we did it, we can repeat it and improve on it. The reverse is true for failures. If we can figure out how we failed, we can avoid making the same errors again.

Focusing on the process and the result will happen may seem like a statement of the blindingly obvious, but we do the opposite a lot, so it’s worth talking about.

Have you ever got so caught up trying to close a sale that you hurry your discussions and talk about your product/service rather than listening to the client? And yet in our heart of hearts we know that if we build rapport, understand the customer’s drivers and present real benefits to them, the sale will take care of itself.

Or

When you’re trying to get the kids to school on time in the morning. Do you find yourself saying “hurry up, hurry up” for the umpteenth time and still screeching to the school gates in a Formula One fluster? We’re repeating activities that we know don’t work.

So how did Martin help the team to become more process-driven in their approach and how can we use the method to make our lives easier?

The crew’s process driven approach divides into three sections:

1.Get curious about the recipe

2.Focus your attention

3.Measure success

1. Get curious about the recipe

•What gets you one result rather than another?

•What are the variables that might be having an impact?

Most people are oblivious to the causes that get them their results. Simply by waking up and wondering, you are one step ahead of the competition.

For example, I’m not going to appear on Masterchef anytime soon – in fact my son commented the other day,

“Daddy cooks stuff and puts it in the fridge and then Mummy puts it in the microwave until it goes ping.”

But because Delia went to the trouble of analysing and writing down the ingredients and the recipe for making a cake, even I can benefit from her curiosity and make half decent cupcakes for an annoyingly perceptive four year old.

Martin got curious about what the British rowing 8+ should be focused on. He looked at other teams and analysed their performance. He talked to the crew, he used his intuition, all to try and figure out what might be the key ingredients and recipe for excellent rowing.

Ben: “Most sports are about rhythm and timing and in a repetitive sport like rowing, rhythm is king. Martin devoted a whole year to getting a rhythm that was more economical and faster than anyone else’s.”

What might this step look like in our world?

Take the goal of getting the kids to school on time; if we focused on the process, rather than the result, what might be some of the important variables to look at? For me it would be type of breakfast, location of clothing, my attitude.

- Rice Krispies can be eaten one grain at a time, but Shreddies are a bit speedier.

- Getting really cross and demanding my children put clothes on takes ages, but asking Fireman Jack to put his special protective uniform on is much quicker.

Now I have some recipes I can work on to increase the chances of achieving my goal – as opposed to getting more and more frustrated by just wishing for the goal really hard.

2. Focus your attention

There’s a weird truth about focusing our attention: as long as we’re purely focusing our attention and not judging or beating ourselves up, then just being aware of a key variable raises our performance.

Ben: “When Rowley was coxing us he was constantly reminding us to focus on specific things like moving our hands or getting our weight on our feet. He usually didn’t need to tell us to make a change – once he’d focused our attention on the variable the change would just happen.”

Try it out yourself! For the things that don’t get sorted out automatically, focusing your attention gives you a crystal clear understanding of what to improve.

We coached a management trainer who wanted to get better feedback from his delegates. He figured out that a key ingredient was the quality of the questions he asked the group during a session. Before we did any training on how to ask better questions he simply focused his attention on the questions he asked in his next session. The quality of the questions dramatically improved – as did the feedback scores. Just raising his awareness to an important variable was enough to improve his performance noticeably.

Every three-five weeks the crew would have planning meetings to agree what specific element they would focus on for the next few weeks. This then translated into agreed learning goals for every single daily training session.

Ben: “Martin made sure each session had a point, it wasn’t just about getting the mileage done.”

This was pretty revolutionary. The received wisdom was that, in order to win, you needed to get the fastest rowers you could find and put them through a brutal training programme (of lots of mileage) to make them faster still.

Ben: “I remember a session way back in perhaps 97, before Martin was involved in the team. It was the first session back after Christmas and there were just four of us there. We didn’t think about what we wanted to learn, we just blundered on in. We took the quad out because it is the fastest boat and so we could get the training over and done with most quickly. We had a weight session and then we went for a run which involved me getting pushed in for an unscheduled swim in the river. I’m not sure what we achieved because we hadn’t set ourselves a goal up front other than ‘do training’.”

How might ‘focusing attention’ look in our world? Say we wanted to improve our team meetings. We’ve already got curious about the recipe and think that the key ingredients are clarity of agenda, timekeeping, and good listening. We then need to set some learning goals for the next team meeting. For example, we agree that we’re going to focus our attention on listening in the next session whilst we are progressing through the agenda.

Ben and the crew had Martin there during both training sessions and races to help them keep focused. For example, if the training session was on the ergo, Martin would be all over the crew picking apart every bit of what was happening. He’d get them to focus on the noise the fan was making, or how they were pushing off their feet, or the angle of their body as they pulled.

In the corporate world, managers and peers can play a big part. For example, in a sales team, does the manager judge his guys purely on sales, or on the process they used to get those sales? How can peers remind each other to keep focused? For instance, we’ve witnessed colleagues using code words in client meetings to remind each other what to focus on. Even those who work alone can write notes to themselves, set the alarm on their phone or put reminders in their calendar.

Ben: “It was very easy to get distracted. For example, Harry was passionate and would get wrapped up in the moment. He’d go off on a tangent in the middle of a training session. Martin would warn us in advance to keep focused on the pre-agreed training goals for that day. You get what you focus on. You don’t have to work harder; it’s not through doing more hours, it is by being more focused within those hours.”

3.Measure success

While other crews were measuring success in terms of number of Regattas won, Ben and his crew were measuring success in terms of how good they were getting at the process.

Ben: “Before the world championships in 1998 we’d stand around before a training session going, “All right?” “Yeah.” Then at the end of the session “All right?” “Yeah.” That was pretty much the sum total of our thoughts on process, but Martin made us realise that a result is in the past as soon as it has happened. The result doesn’t matter, the performance that got you there does.”

So success then becomes…

•Were we successful in focusing on whatever variable we agreed up front that we’d focus on – or did we get distracted? For example the speed of the oar through the water or asking our client questions in a sales meeting or focusing on timing in a team meeting.

•Were we right that certain variables would impact the end result? Did maintaining the same speed of the oar through the water make the boat go faster? Did changing Rice Krispies to Shreddies get the kids to school on time? By sending out pre-work before the team meeting did we have a more productive session?

How can we measure success? In the gym the crew had plenty of fancy statistics they could look at, and in our lives there may well be concrete measures you can use. However, when the crew had training sessions on the water they began to rely more and more on “what does it feel like?”.

Ben: “For the five and a half minutes of an Olympic final, we couldn’t rely on the coach’s feedback or data from machines. We had to put everything into the rowing itself. So we had to get really good at measuring our performance ourselves, in the moment.”

Imagine you are giving a corporate presentation. How can you measure your performance? Feedback forms at the end of the session are one measure of success and can help improve the process for next time. But what about being able to measure during the session if you are on or off track, to drive better performance now. What might be some evidence? Nodding heads? Questions asked? Eyelids open?

How it worked for the team

It took Martin a while to convince the team to be process driven in their approach, but by 1999 they’d signed up to it and the first real test of whether being process driven worked was at the first 1999 Regatta in Hazewinkel, Belgium. Even then, Ben found it really difficult to let go of measuring success in terms of winning.

Ben: “We spent ages in our planning meeting discussing what our focus for Hazewinkel should be. I was adamant it should be ‘get a medal’, other boys wanted ‘row a rhythm’ and eventually we went for that. We would focus our attention purely on rowing an effective rhythm, purely on the process. Of course, we all wanted a medal, but we’d give ourselves the best chance of one if we focused all our effort and energy on the process – on the rhythm. During the race we were totally absorbed in the process – we rowed a really good rhythm and we could feel it. We were so focused on it that some of the boys were totally unaware we’d won a medal!”

Conclusion

Focus on the process and success will take care of itself. All too often we get bent out of shape trying to reach a result – get to a meeting on time, close a sale, get great feedback – and lose sight of what we need to do in order to achieve that result – ask directions, build rapport, present a compelling case.

Our attention is an incredibly powerful tool. When we focus our attention on a key variable (and remember this doesn’t mean beating ourselves up!) then our performance automatically improves and we identify what further support we need. A win is in the past as soon as it has happened, so we need to measure ourselves in terms of our understanding of the process and our execution of the key variables. When we do this we massively increase our chances of winning consistently on an ongoing basis.

Being process-driven puts us on a winning streak. What do we need to do to make this sustainable? The next chapter is all about building momentum.
