Leadership and the One Minute Manager

Ken Blanchard, Patricia Zigarmi & Drea Zigarmi — a condensed reading experience

Design Note

This is a business parable, and in a parable the sequence is the argument: the entrepreneur cannot understand matching until she has met the people, learned to diagnose development levels, and watched a single style fail in the wrong place. So navigation is strictly chapter-linear—a sticky scroll-spy TOC that tracks position rather than invites jumping, because skipping ahead breaks the inductive logic the authors built deliberately. Illustration is Channel A only (Mermaid/SVG): the Situational Leadership II model is a system of crisp 2×2 relationships—competence/commitment, directive/supportive, D1→D4 mapped onto S1→S4—that diagrams externalize far more honestly than decorative imagery would; photographs or stock art would add polish without adding comprehension, so they are excluded. Graphic intensity is standard: one diagram at each of the eight concept chapters, placed before the anchors so the reader holds the framework in mind while reading the source language, with pull-quotes every few chapters to give the eye a landing and the memory a hook.

Written as a parable, this is the story of an overworked entrepreneur who learns to stop doing everything herself—and to lead each person differently, according to what that person actually needs.

Written as a parable, it tells the story of an overworked entrepreneur who learns from the One Minute Manager how to get the most out of a diverse team by becoming a situational leader.

4 · Different Strokes for Different Folks ~1 min

If you are going to lead each person differently, you first need a working definition of what a leadership style actually is.

"Your leadership style is the way you work with someone," said the One Minute Manager. "It's how you behave, over time, when you're trying to influence the performance of others, as perceived by them.

5 · Leadership Style: Perceptions of Others ~1 min

To make this concrete, he sends her out to see how his own people experience him.

He sends her to interview his people: Larry, new to his role, is led directively; Cindy, a seasoned finance director, as an equal.

"A situational leader," said John. "He changes his style, depending on the person he is working with and on the situation."

6 · Three Skills of a Situational Leader ~1 min

Situational leadership is not a personality—it is three repeatable skills that feed each other.

So, the three skills are: goal setting, diagnosis, and matching.

7 · Managing the Performance of Others ~1 min

Those skills live inside a larger performance-management loop that runs all year, not just at review time.

We talk about three parts to performance management: 1. Performance Planning 2. Day-to-Day Coaching 3. Performance Evaluation
Once your people are clear on their goals—they have the final exam questions—it's your job to do everything you can to help them accomplish those goals—learn the answers—so that when it comes to performance evaluation—the final examination—they get high ratings—As."

8 · Goal Setting ~2 min

The loop begins with goals—and good goals are SMART.

SMART goals answer these questions: Specific—What exactly is the goal or task? When does it need to be accomplished? Motivating—Is the goal meaningful for the individual? Attainable—Is the goal realistic, reasonable, and achievable? Relevant—Is it meaningful work for the organization? Trackable—How will progress and results be measured and tracked?
"The One Minute Manager now calls it 'seagull management.' After goal setting, seagull managers are never around until you make a mistake. Then they fly in, make a lot of noise, dump on everybody, and fly out."

9 · Diagnosing Development Level ~3 min

Once goals are set, the second skill is diagnosis: reading where each person sits on two axes.

"You need to look at two factors to determine a person's development level: competence and commitment.
Confidence is a measure of a person's self-assuredness—a feeling of being able to do a task well without much direction—whereas motivation is a person's interest in and enthusiasm for doing a task well."
"When you are D1 on a particular goal or task, you are known as an enthusiastic beginner," said Kathy. "Though you have high commitment, you are inexperienced.
"Don't be surprised if you soon become a D2—or what we call a disillusioned learner," said Kathy with a laugh.
"We call D3s capable but cautious contributors. They have demonstrated some competence and experience in doing the task, but they lack confidence in doing that task by themselves.
"If you become a D4 in your learning to be a situational leader you'll be a self-reliant achiever.
Diagnosis comes before direction: you cannot match a style to a person you have not yet read.

10 · Leadership Style Flexibility ~1 min

The third skill is flexibility—having four distinct styles plotted on the directive and supportive axes.

The leader provides specific direction about goals, shows and tells how, and closely monitors the individual's performance in order to provide frequent feedback on results.

11 · The Four Basic Leadership Styles ~1 min

Each axis has its own vocabulary of behaviors.

Four words can be used to define directive behavior: decide, teach, observe, and provide frequent feedback. Different words are used to describe supportive behavior: listen, involve, facilitate, and encourage."

12 · Matching Leadership Style to Development Level ~1 min

Now the two halves of the model meet: a style is right only relative to a person's development level.

"So an S1 directing style is a good match when a decision has to be made quickly and the stakes are high," said the One Minute Manager.
I think people lose their commitment only after they realize that good performance doesn't make a difference."

Coaching fits the disillusioned learner, supporting the cautious performer, delegating the self-reliant achiever; a purely supportive style fails where direction is needed.

13 · Think Before You Act ~1 min

The matching rule pairs each development level with exactly one style.

Delegating (Style 4) is for self-reliant achievers who have both competence and commitment (D4)
They must have an internal critic who keeps telling them, 'Don't Just Sit There—Do Something!'

14 · Different Strokes for the Same Folks ~1 min

Development level is task-specific—so the same person can need two different styles at once.

The same person can need different styles: his son Tom was a D4 in reading but a D2 in math, needing directive coaching.

"People do not learn skills by love alone."
"People do not learn skills by love alone."

15 · Developing Competence and Commitment ~2 min

The leader's job is to move people forward—shifting style as competence and confidence grow.

Your goal as a manager should be to gradually increase the competence and confidence of your people so that you can begin to use less time-consuming styles—supporting and delegating—and still get high quality results."
Since a D1 has commitment but lacks competence, the leader needs to provide direction (S1-Directing) to help build competence; since a D2 lacks both competence and commitment, the leader has to provide both direction and support (S2-Coaching) to reenergize and re-teach; since a D3 has competence but variable commitment, the leader needs to provide support (S3-Supporting) to build confidence in competence; and since a D4 has both competence and commitment, the leader values their contribution by permitting them to provide their own direction or support (S4-Delegating)."
If You Want to Develop People, Catch Them Doing Things Right, Not Wrong.
"If You Want to Develop People, Catch Them Doing Things Right, Not Wrong."

16 · Turning Around Performance Problems ~1 min

When performance slips, the leader steps back in—adding direction rather than withdrawing it.

If you still don't get results," the One Minute Manager continued, "you should move to a coaching style, where you provide some direction, closer supervision, and shorter timelines.

17 · Sharing What You're Doing ~1 min

The model works best in the open—as something done with people, not to them.

positive assumptions about people are a given; you believe people have the potential to become high performers. What fluctuates is the leader's behavior, depending on their people's needs for direction and support."
